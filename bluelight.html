Negative Effects of Blue-light

Computers, phones, tablets, and televisions are our main sources of electronic light.
Of the wavelengths of visible light, blue-light has been attributed to increasing risk of retinal cell death. This systematic review claims that blue light that has high energy and a short wavelength, so due to the photoelectric effect, it can remove more electrons off of molecules and damage the eye by changing its oxygen concentrations (Algvere). They conclude this from observing a plethora of experiments such as animal-model tests on rats, graphs of blue-light exposure time and surviving rod/cone cells, and how susceptibility to blue-light was greater in older rats than younger rats. Interestingly, age has a factor in how significant the ocular damage is to a mammalian eye. Keep in mind that age is a confounding factor for the next few experiments! In terms of validity, this study has an impact factor of 12.38, is peer-reviewed, and is open access. Indeed, the journal is open access, but it is most likely not predatory because of its high impact factor and peer-reviews.
In addition to eye-strain, a study posted in the journal of PNAS has shown that light-emitting electronics before bedtime can have a signficant impact on sleep, morning alertness, melatonin suppression, and the circadian clock. A shifted circadian clock would be like having jet-lag: the body feels like it is not aligned with our daily schedules. Ultimately, this would decrease the amount of sleep one would receive, increasing stress, loss of function, and a shortened life expectancy.
Some pros of this study: They performed a randomized, crossover design where one group read an LE-eBook for about four hours before bedtime for five consecutive evenings, whereas the other group read a printed book in the same dim-light environment. Hourly blood samples were collected to measure melatonin levels. They had adequate experimental design in this experiment.
Some cons of this study: Participants rated their sleepiness/alertness on a computerized scale in the evening and in the following morning, and they only observed 12 young adults (6 men and 6 women). The study would be much more reliable if they could use a different way to test morning alertness (i.e. physical, coordinated excersizes) since sleepiness/alertness in their study is subjective. Increasing participant size along with randomizing the demographics of the participants would make the experiment's conclusions stronger and eliminate confounding variables. In addition, AGE is a confounding factor that was not accounted for in this experiment!
Another scientific journal from NCBI, demonstrates that these negative effects are not visible/harmful in short-term studies (6 months to 1.5 years). For most of these, however, a longitudinal study does not exist. In a world where technology has perpetuated every aspect of our life, the long-term consequences of blue-light should be studied. We don't know how constant blue-light and shifts in our circadian rhythm will effect aging, eye-sight, and stress. One could say that we could compare the eye-tissue and health of people who heavily use screen electronics with those who have not, but there are so many confounding factors such as age, natural sunlight, genetics. I will go into the validity of this experiment later on.

Night-Shift Mode

What is Night-Shift Mode?
Flip the switch above. See how the background shifts into warmer tones? Night-shift mode is essentially a mode where blue-light is minimized on your device screen. Apple and Android products all have this function, but does it actually work?
One study performs a matched-pairs comparing usages of Night-shift mode. They found out that blue-light (no Night-Shift) does encourage melatonin suppression, but melatonin suppression did not significantly differ between the mode being on/off. However, the long-term benefits of using Night-Shift mode still need to be researched.
Studies on other methods of blocking blue-light emission also offer some insight: orange-tinted glasses significantly reduces short wavelength emissions (blue and purple light). Color adjustment apps (performing like Night-Shift mode) are also very effective in reducing these short-wave emissions. Overall, we don't know yet the extent of the adverse effects of blue-light. There is evidence of several negative effects, and we have tools to help reduce these short-term and potentially long-term effects.

Blue-light isn't entirely bad...

There are several good applications of blue-light.

For example, this blue-light mask is used for skin rejuvenation therapy. Blue-light radiation is used to elimnate certain kinds of moles, clear acne, and acne scars. Of course, the blue-light emission from phones and computers are not specific/powerful enough for this.

More specifically related to our circadian rhythms, blue-light therapy is used to fix a misaligned circadian rhythm. The way they went about their experiment was that they placed the particpants in a dark room with cots to sleep in as they manipulated their circadian cycles by changing the lighting and emitting blue-light for 30 min intervals. Although melatonin pills are a convenient solution to this problem, they claim that light therapy has beneficial side effects such as improvements in mood and alertness.

_ Should we be using Night-Mode? _ Blue-light has demonstrated effects of shifting circadian rhythms (for the better or the worst) and ocular cell damage. The short-term effects may not be adverse, but we know nothing about the long term effects. I would argue that there is no harm in using Night-Shift mode so I would use it.
