Negative Effects of Blue-light

Computers, phones, tablets, and televisions are our main sources of electronic light.
Of the wavelengths of visible light, blue-light has been attributed to increasing risk of retinal cell death. Blue light that has high energy and a short wavelength, and due to the photoelectric effect, it can remove more electrons off of molecules and damage the eye by changing its oxygen concentrations (). In addition to eye-strain, studies have shown that light-emitting electronics before bedtime can have a signficant impact on sleep, morning alertness, melatonin suppression, and the circadian clock. A shifted circadian clock would be like having jet-lag: the body feels like it is not aligned with our daily schedules. Ultimately, this would decrease the amount of sleep one would receive, increasing stress, loss of function, and a shortened life expectancy.
From the scientific journals that I have researched, most of them demonstrate that these negative effects are not as visible and harmful in short-term studies (6 months to 1.5 years). For most of these, however, a longitudinal study does not exist. In a world where technology has perpetuated every aspect of our life, the long-term consequences of blue-light should be studied. We don't know how constant blue-light and shifts in our circadian rhythm will effect aging, eye-sight, and stress. One could say that we could compare the eye-tissue and health of people who heavily use screen electronics with those who have not, but there are so many confounding factors such as age, natural sunlight, genetics.

Night-Shift Mode

What is Night-Shift Mode?
Flip the switch above. See how the background shifts into warmer tones? Night-shift mode is essentially a mode where blue-light is minimized on your device screen. Apple and Android products all have this function, but does it actually work?
One study performs a matched-pairs comparing usages of Night-shift mode. They found out that blue-light (no Night-Shift) does encourage melatonin suppression, but melatonin suppression did not significantly differ between the mode being on/off. However, the long-term benefits of using Night-Shift mode still need to be researched.
Studies on other methods of blocking blue-light emission also offer some insight: orange-tinted glasses significantly reduces short wavelength emissions (blue and purple light). Color adjustment apps (performing like Night-Shift mode) are also very effective in reducing these short-wave emissions. Overall, we don't know yet the extent of the adverse effects of blue-light. There is evidence of several negative effects, and we have tools to help reduce these short-term and potentially long-term effects.

Blue-light isn't entirely bad...

There are several good applications of blue-light.

For example, this blue-light mask is used for skin rejuvenation therapy. Blue-light radiation is used to elimnate certain kinds of moles, clear acne, and acne scars. Of course, the blue-light emission from phones and computers are not specific/powerful enough for this.

More specifically related to our circadian rhythms, blue-light therapy is used to fix a misaligned circadian rhythm. The way they went about their experiment was that they placed the particpants in a dark room with cots to sleep in as they manipulated their circadian cycles by changing the lighting and emitting blue-light for 30 min intervals. Although melatonin pills are a convenient solution to this problem, they claim that light therapy has beneficial side effects such as improvements in mood and alertness.

_ Should we be using Night-Mode? _ Blue-light has demonstrated effects of shifting circadian rhythms (for the better or the worst) and ocular cell damage. The short-term effects may not be adverse, but we know nothing about the long term effects. I would argue that there is no harm in using Night-Shift mode so I would use it.
